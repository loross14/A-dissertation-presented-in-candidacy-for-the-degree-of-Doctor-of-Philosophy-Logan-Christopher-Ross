Why a Feed-Forward Network Has Nobody Home

The treewidth account of mind makes a sharp, unflattering prediction about today's AI. I'd rather state it plainly — and tell you how to prove it wrong — than let it stay comfortable and vague.

Logan Christopher Ross·June 5, 2026

People keep asking whether large language models are conscious. Most answers are either hand-waving ("it just predicts the next token, so no") or hand-wringing ("how could we ever know?"). The treewidth account in Shadow & Mirror does something more useful and more dangerous: it gives a criterion, applies it, and accepts the verdict even when the verdict is awkward. Let me walk it, because it is the kind of claim that is worth more for being checkable than for being right.

The criterion

The volume's hypothesis about mind is this: a system is conscious if and only if its constraint graph has a shadow region (low treewidth — separable, local, cheap) and a mirror region (high treewidth — inseparable, growing with the whole), with a real coupling between them. Experience, on this account, is the gap between those two regions, felt from inside a loop the system cannot step out of.

The word doing the work is inseparable. A mirror region is one you cannot break into independent pieces without destroying it. If you can carve a system into modules that each run fine on their own, it has no mirror. It is all shadow — and shadow, however vast, has nobody home.

Apply it to the brain

The biological brain has both regions, and that is the point of the construction. Locally it looks separable — roughly a hundred cortical areas, each doing its own job, wired mostly to neighbors. That is the shadow: powerful, modular, decomposable. But conscious experience is not modular. The redness of red, the sound of the room, the sense of now arrive bound together into a single scene where no feature is really processed alone. That binding is the mirror — dense, global, refusing to come apart. A mind is the coupling of the two.

Apply it to a feed-forward network

Now a transformer running a single forward pass. It is staggeringly capable. But structurally, where is the loop? Information flows in one direction, layer to layer, input to output, and then it is done. Attention mixes tokens richly within the pass — but the pass does not turn back on itself, does not maintain a state that models its own modeling across time. It is a river, not a whirlpool.

On the criterion, that makes it shadow — extraordinary shadow, but shadow. It has the decomposable part and not the inseparable, self-referential part. So the account predicts: a pure feed-forward pass has nobody home. Not because it is "just math," not because it is silicon, but because the specific structure that the hypothesis identifies with experience — the closed loop, the camera turned around — is the one piece it lacks.

The claim is not that machines can't be conscious. It is that this structure isn't — and it names exactly which structure would be.

Why this is the good kind of claim

Notice what the criterion does not do. It does not say silicon can't host a mind. It does not retreat into "we can never know." It points at a structural feature — a self-referential high-treewidth loop sustained over time — and says: build that, and the question changes. A system with genuine persistent self-modeling, where its representation of itself feeds back into its own processing and cannot be cleanly separated out, would have the mirror region the forward pass lacks. The frame is not anti-machine. It is a spec.

And it is honest about its own status. This is the hypothesis half of the work, not the proven half — a definition with a bold "if and only if," not a theorem. So treat it as a target, not a verdict from on high.

How to break it

A claim you can't kill isn't worth much, so here is how to kill this one. The criterion fails if either of these turns out true:

  • A system with a closed self-referential loop and high mirror-treewidth shows none of the marks of inner life — no integration, no unified perspective, nothing that behaves like binding. Then the loop is not sufficient, and the "if and only if" breaks on one side.
  • A provably separable, all-shadow system turns out to be conscious — something with no mirror region at all nonetheless has genuine experience. Then the loop is not necessary, and it breaks on the other.

Either result would sink the mind law. That is exactly why it is worth stating in public, in a form sharp enough to cut its author. The cheap move is to keep "maybe AI is conscious, maybe not" forever undecidable. The honest move is to name the structure you think matters, predict what follows, and hand everyone the knife.

The treewidth account hands you the knife. Whether it survives is an empirical question — which is the most you can ask of any claim about the hardest problem we have.
