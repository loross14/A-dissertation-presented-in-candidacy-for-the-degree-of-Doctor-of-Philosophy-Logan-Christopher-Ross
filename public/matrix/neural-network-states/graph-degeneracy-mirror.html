How the constraint arises

Neural-Network Quantum States is, structurally, a correlation graph in which units are vertices and weight couplings are edges. A neural-network ansatz represents a quantum state's correlations; its classical tractability is set by the entanglement — and therefore the treewidth — it is forced to carry.

Constrained by graph degeneracy — the densest local core, a cheap lower bound on width — the operative question is the boundary at which a quantum representation becomes necessary. In the mirror regime the graph degeneracy reads d(G) = Ω(N), dominated by volume-law correlations the ansatz cannot compress. This is the irreducible core that makes the boundary at which a quantum representation becomes necessary a hard wall — classical simulation pays exp(Θ(tw(G))) and loses.

The Treewidth Gap

This is the mirror — the inseparable whole. Its separable shadow, where the same graph degeneracy stays bounded, is the Shadow regime of neural-network quantum states under graph degeneracy. The gap between them is what treewidth measures.

Questions

Is neural-network quantum states classically simulable in the mirror regime?

No. With d(G) = Ω(N) the width grows with input size, so by Ross's Law classical cost is exp(Θ(tw(G))) and a quantum representation is required.

What does d(G) = Ω(N) mean for neural-network quantum states?

It is the graph degeneracy of the system's graph in this regime &mdash; the densest local core, a cheap lower bound on width. That value is the exponent governing simulation cost.

How does Ross's Law apply here?

Ross's Law states simulation cost scales as exp(Θ(tw(G))). With boundary condition d(G) = Ω(N), that places neural-network quantum states in a quantum simulation profile.
