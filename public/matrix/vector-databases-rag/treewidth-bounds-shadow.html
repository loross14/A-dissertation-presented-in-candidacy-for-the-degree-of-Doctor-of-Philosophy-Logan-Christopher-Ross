How the constraint arises

Vector Databases & RAG is, structurally, a retrieval graph in which chunks are vertices and semantic-similarity links are edges. A retrieval-augmented index is a similarity graph; the cost of coherent multi-hop retrieval tracks the treewidth of the activated subgraph, not its raw size.

Constrained by treewidth bounds — how far the structure is from a tree — the exponent in Ross's Law — the operative question is why retrieval quality collapses as the context grows densely linked. In the shadow regime the treewidth bounds reads tw(G) = O(1), so the graph is effectively tree-like and why retrieval quality collapses as the context grows densely linked simply does not bite: a classical pass resolves it in near-linear time.

The Treewidth Gap

This is the shadow — the separable projection. Its inseparable counterpart, where the same treewidth bounds grows without bound, is the Mirror regime of vector databases & rag under treewidth bounds. The gap between them is what treewidth measures.

Questions

Is vector databases & rag classically simulable in the shadow regime?

Yes. With tw(G) = O(1) the structure is effectively tree-like, so a classical method resolves it efficiently.

What does tw(G) = O(1) mean for vector databases & rag?

It is the treewidth bounds of the system's graph in this regime &mdash; how far the structure is from a tree — the exponent in Ross's Law. That value is the exponent governing simulation cost.

How does Ross's Law apply here?

Ross's Law states simulation cost scales as exp(Θ(tw(G))). With boundary condition tw(G) = O(1), that places vector databases & rag in a classical simulation profile.
